Yoga Therapy

Taking away from Patanjali’s Yoga Sutra and Bhagawad Gita, Yoga is defined as…

‘Chitta Vritti Nirodha’, stilling of mind in order to attain the ultimate reality and move toward self-realization;

‘Karmasu Kaushalam’, excellence in action and work; ‘Samatva Uchatam’, bringing the state of mind and body to a state of even-mindedness.

The definition of Yoga in itself highlights the importance of Yoga in one’s life. The original body-mind medicine, Yoga has been mentioned in the oldest of Hindu scriptures as a way of life, that which cannot be removed from the process of life.

For many years, Yoga itself has proved to be therapeutic and healing for it understands the influence of body and mind on the self – it cleanses, steadies, harmonizes, stabilizes, calms, relaxes and restored the imbalances in our mind-body. More so, Yogic concepts and techniques such as asanas, pranayama, dhyana (meditation), and more help us to develop the right attitudes toward life.

The cornerstone of Yoga therapy is the Panchakosha model that suggests that a human being experiences life in 5 sheaths

1. Annamaya kosha – the food sheath: the physical body that requires food and nourishment to thrive.

2. Pranamaya kosha – the breath sheath: exists within the physical body and flows in the circulatory, lymphatic and nervous systems.

3. Manomaya kosha – the mind sheath: governs perception of the world and develops a sense of the Self.

4. Vijnanamaya kosha – the intellect sheath: the conscious layer that governs the sense of morals and ethics that are responsible for inner growth and gaining knowledge.

5. Anandamaya kosha – the bliss sheath: uniting all the koshas and experiencing unity with consciousness deeper than the materialistic world.
